# A guide to modern punctuation

Use as little punctuation as necessary to convey meaning. If a sentence needs a lot of punctuation to make sense, rewrite it.

---

## Apostrophes

Apostrophes do two jobs: show possession and mark missing letters in contractions.

### Possession

**Singular nouns**: add *'s*, even when the noun ends in *s*—unless the result sounds awkward.

- *Sandra's book*
- *James's thesis*
- *Socrates' life* (sounds better without the extra *s*)

If pronunciation is ambiguous, rearrange the sentence: *The thesis James submitted was longer than expected.*

**Plural nouns ending in *s***: add only an apostrophe.

- *performers' entrance*
- *delegates' lunch*

**Plural nouns not ending in *s***: add *'s*.

- *the women's team*
- *children's books*

**Compound nouns and joint possession**: put the apostrophe on the final element.

- *Mitchell and Webb's latest show*
- *the Sheriff of Nottingham's appointment*

**Time periods**: use an apostrophe when the time period acts as a possessive (test by substituting *of*).

- *three months' notice* (notice of three months)
- *this year's agenda* (the agenda of this year)

Don't use an apostrophe when the time period modifies an adjective: *She was eight months pregnant.*

**Its**: the possessive form of *it* has no apostrophe. *The system restarted its services.* Reserve *it's* for the contraction of *it is* or *it has*.

**Plurals**: never use an apostrophe to form a plural. *APIs*, not *API's*. *The 1990s*, not *the 1990's*. The single exception is the plural of individual lowercase letters: *dot the i's and cross the t's*.

### Contractions

Use an apostrophe where the missing letters would have been: *don't, it's, you're, we're, can't*.

## Commas

### Always use a comma

- **Before the conjunction in a list of three or more items** (the serial/Oxford comma). This prevents ambiguity.
  - *eggs, flour, milk, and red wine*

- **After an introductory phrase**.
  - *With the new CLI, you can deploy in seconds.*

- **To join independent clauses with a conjunction** (*and, or, but, so*).
  - *Select Options, and then select Enable.*

- **Between qualifying adjectives** that could be reversed or joined with *and*.
  - *a large, friendly llama*

- **Around a word or clause that can be removed** without losing the sentence's meaning.
  - *Sir Clive Granger, a University of Nottingham graduate, won the 2003 Nobel Prize.*

- **Around transitional words** like *however, though, of course, nevertheless*.
  - *It was far too late, though, to worry about the meeting.*

### Never use a comma

- **To join independent clauses without a conjunction.** Use a semicolon or split into two sentences.
- **Between verbs in a compound predicate.** *The program evaluates your system and copies the files.*
- **Between classifying adjectives** (category, origin, age, material). *A 14-year-old South American llama.*

---

## Semicolons

Semicolons signal complexity. Before reaching for one, try splitting the sentence or converting it to a list.

Use a semicolon:

- **Between two independent clauses not joined by a conjunction.** *Some reporters were brilliant; others were mediocre.*
- **To separate items in a series that already contains commas.** Even better, convert the series to a bulleted list.

---

## Colons

- **To introduce a list**, when the lead-in text directly references the items (*the following, these,* or a stated number). Otherwise, use a period.
  - *The tool supports three modes: scan, build, and deploy.*
- **To separate a title and subtitle** in a heading. Capitalise the first word after the colon.
  - *Get started with Azure IoT: An interactive guide*
- **To introduce a quoted statement.**
  - *She said: "Our vision is to change the world for the better."*

Don't end a heading, label, or table introduction with a colon. Lowercase the word after a colon in running text unless it's a proper noun.

---

## Full stops (periods)

- End every sentence with a full stop.
- One space after a full stop. Never two.
- Don't use full stops in headings, subheadings, or UI labels.
- **In lists**: use a full stop after each item if any item is a complete sentence. Skip full stops if all items are short phrases.
- Don't place a full stop immediately before or after an ellipsis.

---

## Question marks and exclamation marks

- Use a question mark for direct questions. Use a full stop for reported questions: *They asked if the budget would increase.*
- **Exclamation marks**: avoid them. They rarely add anything in technical or professional writing. Use one only if it's part of an official title.

---

## Quotation marks

- Use **double quotation marks** for direct speech, quotes, and when drawing attention to a specific term on first use.
  - *"What happens next is up for discussion," she said.*
- Use **single quotation marks** for quotes within quotes.
  - *"So I said to him, 'I don't think so.'"*
- Punctuation that belongs to the quote goes inside the quotation marks. Punctuation that belongs to the surrounding sentence goes outside.
- **Multi-paragraph quotes**: open each paragraph with quotation marks, but close only the final paragraph.

---

## Dashes and hyphens

These are three different marks. Don't interchange them.

### Hyphens (-)

Use hyphens to join compound modifiers **before** the noun they modify. Drop the hyphen when the modifier comes after the noun.

- *a world-class degree* → *the degree is world class*
- *a well-established principle* → *the principle is well established*
- *the 24-hour clock* → *the clock runs 24 hours*

Don't hyphenate when the first word is an adverb ending in *-ly*: *a badly prepared speech*, *genetically modified vegetables*.

Use hyphens with prefixes *ex-*, *self-*, and *all-*, and where needed to prevent misreading: *re-released*, *co-ordinate*.

### En dashes (–)

- **For ranges**, with no spaces: *pages 15–32*, *2020–2024*, *the 18–25 age bracket*.
- **As parenthetical dashes in running text**, with a space on each side: *The Hypsilophodon – a small dinosaur – was omnivorous.* This is the preferred alternative to round brackets or a pair of commas for an aside.

### Em dashes (—)

Use em dashes sparingly. In most contexts an en dash with spaces serves the same purpose. Reserve the em dash for attributing a quotation:

*"Knowledge without character." — Mahatma Gandhi*

---

## Ellipsis (…)

Three dots—no more, no fewer.

- **To show omitted text**, use a space before and after: *"A borrowed book is like a guest … you cannot leave it carelessly."*
- **To trail off**, attach directly to the preceding word: *I was beginning to wonder…*

Don't place a full stop immediately next to an ellipsis.

---

## Brackets

### Round brackets (parentheses)

Use to expand or clarify, or to introduce an acronym at first use:

- *It reached a length of 2.3 m (7.5 ft).*
- *The University Executive Board (UEB) made the decision.*

If the entire sentence is inside the brackets, the full stop goes inside too. Otherwise, punctuate outside.

Prefer dashes or rewriting to brackets. If you find yourself using brackets often, your sentences may be carrying too much information.

### Square brackets [ ]

For editorial insertions into quoted text only: *The President remarked that the country had surrendered [sic].*

---

## Bullet points

- Introduce a bulleted list with a colon (or a complete sentence ending in a full stop).
- **If items complete the introductory sentence**, start each with a lowercase letter and skip end punctuation.
- **If items are independent phrases**, start each with a capital letter and skip end punctuation.
- **If any item is a complete sentence**, start every item with a capital letter and end every item with a full stop.
- Never use semicolons, commas, or conjunctions (*and, or*) at the end of list items.
- Make all items parallel in grammatical structure.
- Aim for two to seven items per list.

---

## Ampersands (&)

Spell out *and* in running text. Use an ampersand only when it's part of an official name or brand, or in constrained spaces like breadcrumb trails and navigation.

---

## The @ symbol

Use only in email addresses and social media handles. Never use it to mean *at* in prose.

---

## Spaces

One space after all punctuation. Never two.
